Gambling in Australia

Exploring Australia's gambling behaviour, financial impact, and global position through data-driven insights.

Introduction

Gambling is deeply rooted in Australian culture, spanning casinos, lotteries, racing, and online platforms. Despite its appeal, it has notable social and financial impacts nationwide.

Australia ranks among the top gambling spenders per capita globally, with billions lost each year across states and game types.

This visualisation explores gambling behaviour, spending patterns, and risk exposure to uncover how access, preference, and policy shape Australia's gambling landscape.

Gambling Loss per Adult by Australian State and Territory

Gambling losses across Australia reveal clear regional contrasts, shaped by population density, machine access, and state-level regulation. The variation provides useful insight into how local factors influence gambling behaviour.

Average losses vary widely across Australia's states and territories. New South Wales leads with around $1,550 per adult annually. Queensland and Victoria follow with roughly $1,150 and $1,050 respectively.

Western Australia records the lowest at about $670 per adult. Tasmania and South Australia fall below the national average. The Northern Territory shows elevated per-capita losses despite small population.

The pattern highlights that greater gambling access and higher population density link with higher losses per adult across regions.

Expenditure Trends by Game Type

Gambling expenditure in Australia has evolved significantly over the past two decades, reflecting economic shifts and technological change. The long-term trend offers insight into how digital access continues to reshape gambling behaviour.

The data also captures key turning points — a steady climb during the 2000s, a temporary dip around 2020 caused by COVID-19 lockdowns, and a rapid post-pandemic rebound as venues reopened and online gambling surged amid growing digital convenience.

Gambling expenditure in Australia has risen sharply since 1999, reaching over $32 billion in 2024. Gaming machines dominate spending, showing strong post-2020 recovery.

Wagering records the fastest growth, boosted by online betting platforms and mobile access. Casinos and lotteries remain stable, while minor gaming declines steadily.

Overall, the data shows a shift toward digital gambling, with online activity reshaping how Australians gamble and spend.

Global Gambling Revenue Comparison

Gambling revenue varies widely across the world, shaped by market size, culture, and regulation. Comparing these figures highlights how economic and social factors influence national gambling industries.

The United States leads the world, generating over $67 billion USD in annual gambling revenue — far exceeding all other countries.

The United Kingdom, France, and Canada follow with earnings between $12-18 billion USD each.

Australia ranks mid-level globally at about $10 billion USD, reflecting high per-capita spending despite its smaller population.

These figures reveal that gambling remains a major global economy, driven by accessibility, technology, and evolving regulation.

Gambling Preferences by Age Group

Gambling habits in Australia differ sharply by age, shaped by technology, lifestyle, and income levels. Younger adults are drawn to online and fast-paced formats, while older groups prefer traditional, low-risk games.

Gambling participation peaks among 30-49 year olds, who show the broadest engagement across all gambling types.

18-29 year olds lean toward sports betting and private betting, reflecting a preference for fast, interactive forms of gambling.

50-64 year olds maintain high involvement in lotteries and gaming machines (EGMs), while the 65+ group shows a decline across all types.

The overall trend highlights that gambling intensity is strongest in mid-life, when financial capacity and risk appetite are highest.

Gambling Activity vs Risk Group

Gambling activity differs notably between risk groups, showing how spending patterns shift from casual play to high-intensity gambling. This relationship offers insight into how risk level influences behaviour.

Non-problem gamblers show the highest participation in lotteries (84%) and gaming machines (60%), reflecting controlled, recreational play.

Low-risk and moderate-risk groups engage moderately across most activities, especially in casino games and race betting.

Problem gamblers spend heavily on gaming machines (61%) and sports betting (47%), highlighting a shift toward high-intensity forms of gambling.

The heatmap reveals that as risk severity rises, gambling behaviour becomes more concentrated in faster, higher-stakes activities.

Sports Betting by Type

Sports betting has surged in popularity, especially among young to middle-aged Australians who are highly active on digital and mobile platforms.

AFL dominates with about 58% participation, followed by NRL (49%),both reflecting Australia's deep-rooted sporting culture.

Other major betting choices include soccer (28%), cricket (27%), and basketball (25%), supported by global tournaments and online accessibility.

The data highlights how digital wagering and real-time sports engagement continue to drive gambling growth among younger demographics.
